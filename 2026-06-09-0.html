Mark Pincus's Proven-Better-New Framework: The Mental Model That Killed Zynga's Overconfidence and Can Save Your Product Roadmap

Farnam Street · 8 min
Zynga's founder revealed the anti-innovation trap that kills startups: believing everything must be revolutionary. His 'Proven, Better, New' framework emerged from $200M+ in failed experiments. The tension: founders confuse novelty with value, burning cash on untested ideas when customers desperately need reliable execution of known wins. This mental model forces intellectual honesty about what's actually differentiated versus what's table stakes.

What Happened

Mark Pincus codified a decision-making framework born from Zynga's chaotic growth and subsequent decline. He categorizes every product decision as Proven (copied best practices), Better (incremental improvements), or New (genuine innovation). The insight: most successful products are 70% Proven, 25% Better, 5% New. Pincus admits Zynga's failures came from inverting this ratio, chasing novelty when they should have been executing fundamentals better than competitors.

Business Model Breakdown

The framework reveals why capital-efficient SaaS wins: Proven features reduce development risk and CAC (customers understand them), Better features justify premium pricing (10-30% improvement = defendable margin), New features create PR and word-of-mouth but rarely drive revenue in year one. The economic logic: stack your bets on Proven execution, use margin from Better to fund selective New experiments.

What Makes Them Succeed

Pincus's unfair advantage was recognizing that innovation theater destroys more value than it creates. While competitors burned resources on moonshots, he could look at FarmVille's success and admit it was 90% Proven (virtual goods from Asian games, appointment mechanics from MMOs) and 10% Better (accessibility, social graph integration). This intellectual honesty let Zynga scale to billions before the hubris set in.

First Principles

Value creation is orthogonal to novelty. Customers pay for outcomes, not originality. The most defensible businesses combine exceptional execution of boring fundamentals with narrow, strategic innovation in high-leverage areas. Risk compounds exponentially when you stack unproven assumptions—every 'New' element multiplies your failure probability.

Why It Matters to You

EasyBuzz operates in a market where Malaysian SMEs are 3-5 years behind on video adoption—they need Proven solutions, not bleeding-edge AI. Janice's pricing ($29-$249) serves businesses that can't afford custom experimentation; they need reliability. The framework reveals where to compete: video templates and workflows are Proven (copy Canva's approach), Malaysian language/cultural customization is Better (justifies premium over global tools), and AI scene detection might be New (high-risk, consider delaying).

What to Steal

Audit your roadmap this week: label every feature Proven/Better/New. If you're above 20% New, you're burning runway on science experiments. Cut ruthlessly. For Malaysian SME video needs, steal Proven patterns from Canva, Loom, and CapCut—these are solved problems. Invest your Better budget in localization depth (Bahasa Malaysia voice-overs, Hari Raya templates, SME-specific workflows) where global competitors won't follow. Reserve New for one high-conviction bet maximum, and only after achieving product-market fit on the Proven foundation. When pitching investors or customers, lead with Proven (de-risk them), highlight Better (show differentiation), whisper about New (create intrigue but don't overpromise).
