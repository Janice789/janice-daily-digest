Tony Fadell (iPod, iPhone, Nest) on the Three Generations Rule: Why Your First Version Will Suck and Why That's the Plan

Lenny Newsletter · 8 min
Fadell's 'three generations rule' — every product takes three full iterations to get right — is the antidote to the 'ship fast and iterate' cargo cult. The iPod didn't nail it until the third version. The iPhone didn't hit stride until the 3GS. Nest took three generations to be defensible. The pattern: generation 1 is proof of concept, generation 2 is fixing what you got wrong, generation 3 is the actual product. Most founders quit at 1.5.

What Happened

Fadell walked through the arc of building iPod, iPhone, and Nest, emphasizing that every iconic product took three full cycles to become iconic. He pushed back on the AI-era assumption that software can iterate its way to greatness — hardware constraints taught him that great products require patience, taste, and a willingness to ship things that are incomplete. His thesis: AI will still need screens and physical interfaces, and the companies that understand how to marry software velocity with hardware discipline will win.

Business Model Breakdown

Apple's model wasn't 'ship the perfect iPod on day one' — it was 'ship a good-enough iPod that creates a beachhead, then use the revenue and feedback to fund versions 2 and 3'. The economic insight: the first version's job is not to be profitable; it's to survive long enough to learn what the real product should be. The margin comes in generation 3, when you've removed all the waste and know exactly what customers will pay for.

What Makes Them Succeed

Fadell's edge is taste plus patience — he can tell the difference between 'this is broken' and 'this is incomplete but on the right path'. His unfair advantage: he's built for multiple generations at both Apple and Google, so he knows what the learning curve looks like. The structural force: consumer hardware is unforgiving — you can't patch your way out of a bad industrial design decision. That constraint creates discipline that translates to better software products too.

First Principles

Great products are not discovered; they're refined across multiple generations. The first version's job is to be good enough to attract early adopters who will fund version 2. The second version's job is to fix what version 1 got obviously wrong. The third version's job is to be the thing you should have built in the first place. Most companies quit before generation 3 because they mistake slow initial growth for product failure.

Why It Matters to You

EasyBuzz is at generation 0.8 — you haven't even shipped version 1.0 yet. Fadell's model says: stop trying to build the perfect product before launch. Ship a version 1.0 that does one thing well enough for 100 Malaysian SMEs to pay $29/mo. Use the next 6 months to discover what version 2.0 needs to fix. Version 3.0 (18-24 months from now) is when you'll have the product you think you're building today. The founders who win are the ones who budget for three generations, not one.

What to Steal

Reframe your roadmap: version 1.0 (next 90 days) is 'prove people will pay for this at all', version 2.0 (6 months out) is 'fix the top 3 things customers complain about', version 3.0 (18 months) is 'the product we should have built'. Stop trying to solve every use case in version 1 — pick one job (e.g., 'SME owner creates an Instagram Reel in under 5 minutes') and nail it. Budget your runway for three generations: if you only have 12 months of cash, you'll quit before version 2. Raise or cut burn to get to 24+ months.
