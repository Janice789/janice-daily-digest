NotebookLM's new update will help you build source repository from chat

TechCrunch · 6 min
Google just inverted the knowledge work stack. Instead of 'bring your sources, then query them,' NotebookLM now starts with your question and builds the knowledge base for you using search + AI. This is Berkshire's 'float' concept applied to information: the system does work upfront (finding sources) that compounds value over time (reusable knowledge base). The psychological insight: people don't want better ways to organize what they have — they want the system to construct what they need.

What Happened

NotebookLM upgraded to Gemini 3.5 and added Antigravity-powered research capabilities. Key changes: users can now start projects by asking questions before adding sources — the AI suggests and retrieves relevant materials via Google Search. The tool generates outputs in multiple formats (charts, Excel, PowerPoint, markdown) with detailed reasoning traces showing how it arrived at answers. Previously required users to upload all sources first. Rolling out to Google AI Ultra and Workspace business customers.

Business Model Breakdown

NotebookLM is a retention + upsell mechanism for Google's AI stack. Free tier builds habit and generates training data, then converts users to Google AI Ultra ($29.99/month) or Workspace AI tiers ($30-35/user/month) when they need advanced research features. The business model is: (1) make AI research indispensable for knowledge workers, (2) lock them into Google's ecosystem via accumulated knowledge bases, (3) upsell adjacent Google services (Drive, Docs, Meet) since their research is already there.

What Makes Them Succeed

Google has the unfair advantage of integrated search + AI + storage in one platform. When NotebookLM suggests sources, it's drawing from Google's index. When it exports to Sheets/Docs, there's zero friction. Competitors like Notion or Mem.ai have better note-taking UX, but they can't match Google's ability to: (1) find sources globally, (2) process them at scale via Gemini, and (3) export to the tools businesses already use daily. The moat is infrastructure breadth, not feature depth.

First Principles

The unit of knowledge work is shifting from documents to queries. Documents were the atomic unit when storage/retrieval was expensive — you organized information upfront to minimize future search cost. Now that AI can synthesize on-demand, the atomic unit becomes the question itself. Systems that start with the question and work backwards (finding sources, organizing them, generating outputs) will beat systems that make users do upfront organization work. This is why Notion is adding AI and Google is pushing NotebookLM — both racing toward query-first knowledge work.

Why It Matters to You

This reveals how AI is changing what 'research tools' mean for your customers. Malaysian SMEs don't want 'better video editing software' — they want 'help me create a video that drives sales.' EasyBuzz should think like NotebookLM: start with the business outcome (generate more leads, showcase products, train staff) and work backwards to content creation. Your competitors (Canva, CapCut) still make users start with a blank canvas. If EasyBuzz can ask 'what's your business goal?' and suggest video templates + content based on that, you're playing a different game. The mental model: your product isn't a tool, it's a consultant that creates tools.

What to Steal

1. Add a 'Start with your goal' onboarding flow: Ask new users 'What do you want this video to achieve?' (options: showcase product, recruit staff, announce promotion, train team). Then pre-populate templates, scripts, stock footage based on their answer. You're doing the 'source building' for them. 2. Show your reasoning: When EasyBuzz suggests a template, show a simple explanation ('This template works for restaurants because it highlights food close-ups in the first 3 seconds'). Makes users feel smart and trust your suggestions. 3. Export flexibility matters: Malaysian SMEs use WhatsApp, Facebook, TikTok, Instagram differently. One video project should export optimized versions for each platform (vertical for Stories, square for Facebook, horizontal for YouTube). NotebookLM's multi-format export is the right mental model.

What Happened

Are AI chatbots making us lose control of our brains?

MIT Tech Review · 7 min
Gloria Mark has spent 30 years studying how digital tools reshape human cognition — and AI chatbots are different. Previous technologies (email, social media, smartphones) fragmented our attention; AI actively replaces our thinking process. The danger isn't distraction, it's delegation: when chatbots generate our drafts, summarize our reading, plan our days, we're outsourcing the cognitive work that builds mental models. This is the Berkshire Hathaway problem in reverse — instead of compounding knowledge, we're compounding dependency. The implication: whoever controls the AI layer controls what people think is possible.

What Happened

Gloria Mark, UC Irvine psychologist and 30-year researcher of human-technology interaction, discussed at SXSW London how AI chatbots differ from previous digital technologies. While email and social media fragmented attention through interruptions, AI chatbots are changing cognition more fundamentally by replacing thinking processes. Instead of being distracted from tasks, users are delegating tasks entirely — drafting, summarizing, planning — which means they're not developing the mental models that come from doing the work themselves. Mark suggests this creates a dependency loop where users increasingly rely on AI for cognitive tasks they previously performed.

Business Model Breakdown

This isn't a company story, but the economic logic is critical: AI companies (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google) monetize by making themselves indispensable to knowledge work. The business model is: (1) free tier builds dependency ('ChatGPT writes my emails now'), (2) paid tier ($20-30/month) locks in users who can't go back to doing it manually, (3) enterprise tier ($30-60/user/month) captures organizations once individual dependency becomes institutional. The unit economics work because marginal cost per query is low (~$0.01-0.10) while switching costs are high (users lose their accumulated prompts, chat history, mental model of how to work with the AI).

What Makes Them Succeed

AI chatbots succeed because they exploit the same cognitive vulnerability that makes all addictive technologies work: they provide immediate relief from effortful thinking. Drafting an email is hard; asking ChatGPT to draft it is instant. The neurological reward is powerful. Unlike social media (which exploits novelty-seeking) or smartphones (which exploit FOMO), AI chatbots exploit our desire to avoid cognitive load. The moat is habit formation — once users experience the relief of not thinking, going back to manual cognitive work feels unnecessarily painful.

First Principles

Cognitive capacity is built through effortful practice. When you write, you build mental models. When you summarize, you build understanding. When you plan, you build judgment. Outsourcing these activities to AI doesn't just save time — it prevents the cognitive development that makes you better at these tasks. This is fundamentally different from previous automation (calculators, spell-check, GPS) because those tools handled narrow, rule-based tasks. AI handles open-ended, creative, judgment-based tasks — precisely the ones that build cognitive capacity. The long-term consequence: a generation of knowledge workers who are functionally dependent on AI for thinking, not just for execution.

Why It Matters to You

Your customers (Malaysian SMEs) are about to face this dependency problem with video content. If EasyBuzz becomes 'AI generates your video from a text prompt,' you create dependent customers, not capable ones. They won't learn what makes good video content — they'll just know 'EasyBuzz does it for me.' This is short-term revenue (they need you) but long-term fragility (they can't evaluate quality, adapt to trends, or switch to competitors offering better AI). The strategic choice: Do you want customers who depend on you (SaaS lock-in) or customers who get better at video because of you (customer success model)? The right answer depends on your values and your market — but understand the trade-off.

What to Steal

1. Design for 'cognitive scaffolding,' not cognitive replacement: Instead of 'AI creates your video,' try 'AI suggests 3 video structures based on your goal — pick one and customize it.' User still makes creative choices, building judgment about what works. This is how Figma beats Canva for designers — it scaffolds creativity rather than replacing it. 2. Show the reasoning, don't hide it: When EasyBuzz's AI suggests a template or edit, show a 1-sentence explanation ('Videos under 15 seconds get 3x more views on Instagram'). This teaches users the 'why' so they get smarter over time. NotebookLM does this with its 'thinking' traces. 3. Create an 'expert mode' that turns off AI suggestions: Let advanced users work manually. This serves two purposes: (a) prevents your power users from feeling infantilized, and (b) signals to newer users that there's mastery to aspire to. Peloton does this well — you can ignore the instructor and do your own workout, which paradoxically makes people trust the instructor more.
