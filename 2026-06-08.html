Available to Google AI Ultra and Workspace business customers first.

First Principles

Users prompt designs using natural language, AI generates images, users can edit and order products (T-shirts, hoodies, tumblers, etc.) fulfilled through Amazon's Merch on Demand print-on-demand service. Unit economics benefit from existing logistics infrastructure—no new warehouses or shipping networks needed. The AI feature drives incremental transactions that feed the fulfillment flywheel.

Are AI chatbots making us lose control of our brains?

MIT Tech Review · 7 min
The cognitive outsourcing trap: when AI handles the 'thinking' tasks, humans lose the metacognitive muscle of knowing whether we actually understand something or just recognize the AI's output. Gloria Mark's 30 years studying human-computer interaction reveals the brutal truth—technology doesn't just change how we work, it rewires how we think. The danger isn't AI getting smarter; it's humans becoming intellectually dependent while believing they're augmented. Like GPS making us forget how to navigate, chatbots may make us forget how to reason.

What Happened

At SXSW London, UC Irvine psychologist Gloria Mark discussed 30 years of research on human-digital interaction, focusing on AI chatbots' cognitive impacts. The concern: chatbots may reduce metacognitive awareness—our ability to evaluate our own understanding. When AI does the thinking, users lose the 'productive struggle' that builds comprehension. Similar to how GPS eroded spatial reasoning, constant AI assistance may atrophy critical thinking skills without users realizing degradation is happening.

Business Model Breakdown

This is research/academic work—funded through university grants and conference speaking. The economic insight: companies building AI tools have misaligned incentives. They profit from engagement and task completion, not from user cognitive development. The tension: making AI 'too helpful' (doing all the thinking) maximizes short-term satisfaction but may create long-term dependency and skill erosion. No business model directly monetizes 'making users smarter,' they monetize 'making tasks easier.'

What Makes Them Succeed

Mark's unfair advantage is longitudinal data—30 years of tracking how technologies (email, smartphones, social media, AI) actually changed human behavior over time, not just initial reactions. This reveals second-order effects competitors miss: the slow erosion of skills, attention spans, metacognition. Her insight: technology's impact isn't what it does to tasks, but what it does to the cognitive processes underlying those tasks. Most AI companies optimize task metrics, ignoring cognitive health metrics.

First Principles

Human cognition develops through effortful processing—the 'productive struggle' of working through problems builds neural pathways and metacognitive awareness. When AI shortcuts that struggle, it optimizes for immediate task completion while atrophying the underlying capability. This creates path dependence: the more you rely on AI thinking, the worse you get at recognizing when you're out of your depth. The metacognitive blindspot is insidious: you don't know what you don't know, and AI doesn't tell you that you've stopped developing the skill to know.

Why It Matters to You

EasyBuzz is building AI-powered video creation for SMEs who may lack video marketing expertise. Mark's research is a warning: if your tool does ALL the thinking (choosing shots, writing scripts, editing), SME owners never learn what makes good video content—they become dependent on your tool without developing marketing judgment. This is both opportunity and responsibility. The opportunity: create cognitive scaffolding features that teach while automating. The danger: optimize so hard for 'easy' that you create learned helplessness.

What to Steal

1) Build 'transparent AI' features: when EasyBuzz's AI chooses a video template, show WHY ('product demo videos perform better with 3-second opening hook because Malaysian Facebook users scroll fast'). Turn automation into teaching moments—users learn principles while getting results. 2) Create 'progressive disclosure of complexity': start with full AI automation for beginners, gradually expose creative controls as users gain confidence. Let SMEs 'graduate' from autopilot to co-pilot to manual control. 3) Add 'confidence checks': before publishing, ask 'does this video match your brand voice?' or 'will your customers understand this message?'—force metacognitive pause. Make users think, don't just execute.
