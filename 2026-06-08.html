NotebookLM's new update will help you build source repository from chat

TechCrunch · 4 min
Google just inverted the knowledge work funnel. For two years, NotebookLM required you to bring sources before asking questions — a product designed for people who already know what they're researching. Now it starts with curiosity and builds the repository backward. This isn't just UX polish; it's Google admitting that AI tools must eliminate setup friction to achieve mass adoption. The winners in AI tooling won't be those with the best models, but those who remove every barrier between impulse and insight.

What Happened

Google upgraded NotebookLM to use Gemini 3.5 and fundamentally changed how users interact with it. Previously, you had to import sources (PDFs, videos, notes) before querying. Now you can start with questions, and NotebookLM will use Google Search and AI to suggest and fetch sources in multiple languages. It also added export formats (charts, PowerPoint, Excel, CSV) and 'thinking transparency' showing reasoning steps. Available first to Google AI Ultra users and Workspace business customers.

Business Model Breakdown

Freemium SaaS with enterprise upsell. Free tier drives adoption, premium features (like this update) reserved for Google AI Ultra subscribers and Workspace business plans. Revenue comes from subscription tiers, with the real money in enterprise seats where companies pay per-user for advanced AI capabilities. The bet: get users hooked on free research tools, then monetize through team collaboration and advanced features.

What Makes Them Succeed

Distribution moat through Google Search integration and Workspace ecosystem lock-in. NotebookLM can pull sources using Google's index — a capability no competitor can replicate. The 'bring your sources' barrier kept adoption capped; removing it unlocks casual users. Google also leverages existing billing relationships with Workspace customers, making upsell frictionless. The real advantage: they're not selling AI, they're making existing Google products smarter.

First Principles

Friction is the enemy of adoption, especially for tools requiring behavior change. Most AI products fail because they demand users restructure their workflow before seeing value. NotebookLM originally violated this — asking users to organize sources before experiencing benefits. By letting curiosity come first and building structure afterward, they align with how humans actually think: question first, organize later. The tool now mirrors natural research behavior instead of imposing artificial prerequisites.

Why It Matters to You

EasyBuzz faces the exact same adoption problem: SMEs won't restructure their content workflow to try a new video tool. NotebookLM's pivot is a masterclass in removing setup friction. For EasyBuzz, this means: don't require SMEs to prepare scripts, organize footage, or learn video terminology before seeing results. Let them describe what they want in plain language and generate something immediately. The first 60 seconds of user experience determine if they stay or churn. Malaysian SMEs especially need instant gratification because they're time-poor and skeptical of new tools.

What to Steal

1) Invert your onboarding: Let users describe their desired video outcome first ("I need a product demo for Facebook"), then have EasyBuzz suggest templates, pull stock footage, generate script options — build the 'sources' for them. Don't make them bring raw materials. 2) Add 'discovery mode' where users start with business goals ("I want to increase foot traffic") and EasyBuzz reverse-engineers the video type, format, and distribution strategy. 3) Show 'thinking transparency' — when EasyBuzz generates a video, show the reasoning: "I used energetic music because you're targeting Gen Z" or "I kept it under 30 seconds because it's for Instagram Reels." Transparency builds trust with first-time users who don't understand AI.

Amazon eliminated even that: type a prompt inside the shopping app, and Alexa generates the design AND manufactures AND ships via Prime. This is vertical integration on steroids. The lesson isn't about AI art — it's about collapsing distribution barriers. Amazon is betting that convenience at point-of-purchase beats quality every time.

What Happened

Amazon launched AI-powered custom merchandise design directly in its Shopping app. Users describe what they want to Alexa ("dog portrait on a T-shirt"), the AI generates designs, users can edit them, then order production through Amazon's Merch on Demand service with Prime shipping. Supports T-shirts, hoodies, tumblers, water bottles. U.S. only. The feature is free; customers only pay for the printed product. This competes directly with Redbubble, Bonfire, Spring, Fourthwall, and other print-on-demand platforms.

Business Model Breakdown

Retail arbitrage meets print-on-demand. Amazon makes money on the product sale markup (manufacturing cost + margin) and captures transaction fees. The real business model is attention arbitrage: keep users inside the Amazon ecosystem by offering every possible service, preventing them from visiting competitor sites. By bundling AI design with instant fulfillment, Amazon turns one-time purchases into impulse buys. The unit economics work because they already have manufacturing infrastructure (Merch on Demand), logistics (Prime), and zero customer acquisition cost (users are already shopping).

What Makes Them Succeed

Embedded distribution and zero-friction impulse buying. Users don't have to leave Amazon, create accounts on third-party platforms, or wait weeks for shipping. The entire flow — ideation, design, purchase, delivery — happens in one ecosystem. Amazon also leverages Prime's psychological ownership effect: customers already feel invested in the Amazon relationship, so adding services feels like getting 'more value' rather than switching costs. Their unfair advantage is simply being where purchase intent already exists. Competitors require users to arrive with intent; Amazon captures spontaneous desire.

First Principles

Distribution beats differentiation when the product is commoditized. Custom merch is now commodified — anyone can print on T-shirts. The winner isn't who has better printing or design tools; it's who reduces decision-making friction and captures users at the moment of impulse. Amazon wins because the gap between "I want this" and "I own this" is measured in seconds, not days. This is the Bezos Doctrine: own the customer relationship, then expand into every adjacent category without switching costs.

If EasyBuzz requires SMEs to 'go somewhere else' to make videos, it will lose to tools that embed at the point of need. The second lesson: impulse matters more than perfection for SMEs. Amazon isn't selling design excellence; it's selling instant gratification. EasyBuzz should prioritize speed-to-first-video over feature depth.

The goal: reduce context-switching. 2) Offer 'one-prompt-to-publish' flow: user describes their need ("I need a Ramadan sale video"), EasyBuzz generates it, and provides one-click publish to FB/IG/TikTok — no downloading, re-uploading, or manual posting. 3) Study Amazon's 'edit suggestions' UX — after generating, show clickable actions like "Make it brighter," "Add text overlay," "Change music." Don't make users learn editing terminology; translate their intent into technical parameters.

WhatsApp says it caught new spyware attacks linked to NSO Group in violation of court order

TechCrunch · 5 min
Compliance is theater when incentives don't align. NSO Group was court-ordered to stop targeting WhatsApp users after a 2019 mass-hacking campaign. They continued anyway, pivoting to phishing attacks instead of zero-click exploits — technically different, legally identical. This reveals a fundamental truth: regulatory penalties only work if they exceed the profit from non-compliance. NSO's customers (governments) pay millions per Pegasus license. A $4 million fine is a rounding error. The deeper lesson for founders: when building in regulated or adversarial spaces, assume bad actors will find loopholes. Design for resilience, not just compliance.

What Happened

WhatsApp detected and disrupted new NSO Group spyware campaigns using phishing links to infect users with Pegasus, violating a 2025 permanent injunction. The injunction stemmed from a 2019 lawsuit where NSO mass-hacked 1,400+ WhatsApp users. A jury initially ordered $167M in damages, later reduced to $4M. WhatsApp is now seeking contempt charges. NSO was acquired by U.S. investors in 2025 and is lobbying to be removed from the U.S. Commerce blocklist. Meanwhile, the U.S. has sanctioned other spyware makers like Intellexa while NSO's status remains unchanged.

Business Model Breakdown

NSO operates as B2G (business-to-government) SaaS for surveillance. Governments pay millions per license for Pegasus, which grants remote access to target smartphones. Revenue model is licensing + support contracts, with per-target fees in some cases. The economics are lucrative because governments have near-unlimited budgets for national security tools, and alternatives (building in-house surveillance tech) are prohibitively expensive. NSO's customer concentration risk is high — losing access to Western markets would be catastrophic, hence the aggressive lobbying to lift U.S. sanctions.

What Makes Them Succeed

Monopoly on zero-click smartphone exploits and government relationships. NSO discovered and weaponized vulnerabilities in iOS/Android that even Apple/Google couldn't patch immediately. Their technical moat was temporary (vulnerabilities get fixed), but their customer relationships are sticky — governments can't easily switch surveillance vendors mid-operation. NSO also benefits from opacity: their customers (authoritarian regimes, intelligence agencies) don't publicly disclose tool usage, making impact hard to measure or regulate. The company's survival despite scandals proves regulatory arbitrage works when you serve powerful clients.

First Principles

Security is an arms race, not a solved problem. Every defense creates an incentive to develop better attacks. WhatsApp added encryption, so NSO found zero-click exploits. WhatsApp patched those, so NSO pivoted to phishing. The cycle is infinite because the economic incentive (governments paying millions) exceeds the cost of R&D. For any company operating in adversarial environments, the question isn't 'how do we stop attacks?' but 'how do we make attacks uneconomical or detectable fast enough to minimize damage?' Perfect security doesn't exist; rapid detection and response do.

Why It Matters to You

EasyBuzz will handle sensitive business content for Malaysian SMEs — product launches, financial promos, internal comms. If EasyBuzz gets hacked or data leaks, SMEs will blame you, not the attacker. The NSO case shows that legal remedies are slow, expensive, and often ineffective (court ordered NSO to stop; they didn't). You must design EasyBuzz assuming bad actors will target it: phishing attacks on your team, social engineering to access customer data, competitors trying to steal your AI models. Build security into product design from day one — end-to-end encryption for video uploads, 2FA mandatory, SOC 2 compliance even before enterprise customers demand it. In SEA, trust is fragile and rumors spread fast. One security incident could kill your reputation before you have a chance to respond.

What to Steal

1) Implement 'paranoid defaults' — assume every user account could be compromised. Require 2FA for all users (not optional), auto-logout after inactivity, and email alerts for unusual activity ("Someone logged in from a new device in Vietnam"). Make security invisible but mandatory. 2) Build a 'trust dashboard' for SMEs showing security posture: "Your videos are encrypted, we don't train AI on your content, here's our SOC 2 cert." Malaysian SMEs won't ask for this proactively, but it builds subconscious trust. 3) Create an incident response playbook now, before you need it. If EasyBuzz gets hacked, you need pre-written templates for: notifying customers, pausing service, engaging lawyers, coordinating with press. WhatsApp's fast response (detecting attacks, filing contempt charges) minimized reputational damage. Delayed response would have destroyed trust.
